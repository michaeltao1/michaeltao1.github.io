June saved its biggest week for last. Two separate transactions above ten billion dollars landed within three days of each other, which almost never happens, and each came from a European-linked buyer paying cash. AbbVie moved first, reaching into immunology to plan for a future it can already see coming. Then Germany's Merck KGaA topped it on price with the month's single largest deal. Underneath the giants, Ipsen and Zymeworks did tidy, opportunistic buyouts. This was the crescendo, and it tells you almost everything about where the sector's money and anxiety are pointed.

The Merck KGaA deal deserves its own sentence before we get to the deep dive, because on price it was the largest of the entire month. On June 25, the German group agreed to buy Minneapolis-based Bio-Techne for about $11.3 billion, or $73 per share in cash, a premium of roughly 24 percent to the prior close and around 36 percent to the one-month average. It is Merck KGaA's biggest acquisition since it bought Sigma-Aldrich back in 2014, and it is a bet on the unglamorous but high-margin business of selling the reagents, antibodies, and tools that every other drugmaker needs. The two smaller deals rounded out the week nicely: Ipsen paid up to $1.75 billion for Kartos and its MDM2 inhibitor navtemadlin in myelofibrosis, and Zymeworks spent roughly $929 million to buy Theravance Biopharma for its marketed COPD drug Yupelri, pushing the antibody specialist into commercial-stage respiratory. Any of these would headline a normal week. This week they were the supporting cast.

## Deep dive: AbbVie and Apogee Therapeutics

On June 22, AbbVie agreed to acquire Apogee Therapeutics for approximately $10.9 billion, paying $135.11 per share in cash. It is AbbVie's first acquisition of 2026, and it is a pure immunology play. The prize is a single, ambitious antibody: zumilokibart, also known as APG777, a long-acting monoclonal antibody that targets interleukin-13. Its lead indication is atopic dermatitis, the chronic inflammatory skin disease, and the company sees it reaching into asthma and eosinophilic esophagitis as well. Apogee has described it as a pipeline-in-a-product, and AbbVie clearly agrees.

## Personal Analysis of the AbbVie / Apogee Deal

Every AbbVie strategy conversation eventually comes back to one drug: Humira. It was the best-selling medicine in the history of the industry, and when it lost US patent protection in 2023, AbbVie faced the single largest revenue cliff any pharma company has ever had to climb down. The remarkable thing is that AbbVie mostly solved it, by nurturing two successor immunology drugs, Skyrizi and Rinvoq, into blockbusters that are now the engine of the company. So why, having escaped one cliff, is AbbVie spending eleven billion dollars in cash on another immunology asset. Because it can already see the next cliff, and it is refusing to be caught flat-footed twice.

That is the mindset that makes this deal make sense. Skyrizi and Rinvoq are growing beautifully now, but their own patents run out in the next decade, and AbbVie has clearly decided that the way to protect an immunology franchise is to keep buying the generation that comes after your current winners. Zumilokibart is that next generation. What makes it interesting is not that it hits IL-13, a target the field already knows works, but how it is engineered to be given. The dominant drug in atopic dermatitis today is Dupixent, from Sanofi and Regeneron, a genuine mega-blockbuster that patients inject every two weeks. Zumilokibart is designed as a half-life-extended antibody that could be dosed far less often, potentially only a handful of times a year. In a chronic disease that patients manage for decades, the difference between an injection every two weeks and one every few months is not a rounding error. It is a real reason for a patient to switch and for a payer to listen.

For AbbVie, the advantages line up almost too neatly. It gets a differentiated, potentially best-in-class asset in atopic dermatitis, a market it already sells into and understands intimately. It gets a credible challenger to the category leader on the one axis, convenience, where the leader is most exposed. And it gets all of this dropped straight into what is arguably the most powerful immunology and dermatology commercial operation on the planet. The same sales reps, the same dermatologists, the same payer contracts that carry Skyrizi and Rinvoq can carry zumilokibart. That is what a real synergy looks like. It is not two back offices merging. It is a new product landing in a distribution machine that is already built, already trusted, and already in the room with the right prescribers.

For Apogee's shareholders, the decision writes itself. A small biotech trying to launch a drug into a market defended by Sanofi and Regeneron faces enormous cost and enormous risk. Taking $135.11 per share in cash removes all of it and locks in the win today. And once again, notice the structure. Like GSK a fortnight earlier, AbbVie paid all cash with no contingencies. Two of the three pharma mega-deals in June were structured as full-price, all-cash purchases of assets the buyers believe in, which tells you these companies are competing for a scarce pool of de-risked, differentiated assets and are willing to pay for certainty to win them.

## Conclusion

The AbbVie and Apogee deal is the clearest statement of the whole month's logic. Even a company that already survived the worst patent cliff in industry history is not resting, it is out buying the drugs that will defend its franchise a decade from now, and it is paying full price in cash to do it. Zumilokibart's atopic dermatitis data is now the milestone to watch, because if a few-times-a-year injection can match the standard of care, AbbVie will have bought itself a genuine Dupixent challenger and a bridge to its post-Skyrizi future. Step back and the timing is the real headline. Two deals above ten billion dollars in a single week, from AbbVie and Merck KGaA, capped a first half of 2026 in which biopharma M&A ran well ahead of the prior year. Patent cliffs are creating the urgency, cash-rich balance sheets are supplying the means, and a limited supply of de-risked assets is keeping premiums high. On this evidence, the deal wave is not slowing down. It is still building.
